# Cavendish Cards — Building the Crossings

*An implementation layer for the deck. The cards name what a mind in motion needs; this shows how to build the physical stepping stones that meet it. A companion to the guidebook, for the people shaping the room.*

Adult layer. Nothing here goes on a card.

---- 

## What this is

The deck names a need. This layer builds the crossing.

A child laying *cave, i need a minute, coming back* has handed you more than a feeling — they've handed you a design brief for the space between one place and the next. This is how you answer it: in cushions, light, sightlines, and floor plan.

It sits alongside the guidebook, not inside it. The guidebook describes each card; this describes the room the cards ask for. Neither replaces a child's real support, and none of this is a script for moving children through a day more smoothly on the institution's schedule. See Guardrails.

This is niche construction and *nesting* made concrete. David Gray-Hammond frames nesting as the physical architecture of lily padding — an architecture of continuity, where one state of being shifts into the next without collapse. The transitional-load framing is Tanya Adkin's. The zones are David Thornburg's learning spaces, carried into Cavendish Space.

---- 

## The one principle

**Design the space *between* the zones — not just the zones.**

Most rooms build the destinations — a reading corner, a group table, a quiet den — and leave the crossings to chance. So a child still has to *leap*: loud-and-social to quiet-and-solo, with nothing underfoot. A hostile gap invites a leap too far, too soon. A padded one offers channels to rest in and careful steps to take.

The crossing is the environment's job. Never the child's deficit to overcome.

---- 

## The zones, and the gradient between them

The Places realm is five zones: **the cave** (solitude), **the campfire** (a small, known group), **the watering hole** (open, ambient company), **the library** (reference and depth), and **the habitat** (the whole surround that holds the other four).

Cave, campfire, and watering hole also run along a sociality gradient — inward and solo, to small group, to open. They line up with the interaction moods: the cave with red (not right now), the campfire with yellow (people I know), the watering hole with green (come say hi). Moving along that gradient on your own terms — alone, together, alone again — is intermittent collaboration. The crossings you're padding are the steps along it.

## Building the crossings

Each movement card, paired with the stepping stone that meets it. Cheap builds first; none of this needs a budget.

- **i need a minute** — *a pause-place at the seam.* A beanbag, a windowsill, a cushion where two zones meet, so leaving a group lands somewhere before it arrives at solitude. The pause is part of the crossing, not a detour from it.
- **slowly** — *a graded sensory strip.* Don't butt the watering hole against the cave. Put a step-down between them where the light dims and the sound drops before arrival. Grade the crossing instead of forcing a single leap.
- **not yet / ready now** — *self-paced departure.* No transition on a whole-room clock. A child crosses when they signal, not when the schedule does. Build routes that let them wait, then go, without asking.
- **coming back** — *a held place and a return thread.* Keep the spot, the task, and the welcome ready. A carried token or object travels the crossing with them — a line of continuity between the two banks.
- **stuck** — *a landing spot in the gap.* Somewhere to be *between* pads without being pushed across. Pair it with a what-helps card and let them choose; never shove.
- **watch first** — *an edge perch with sightlines.* A place at the rim of a group, in view of it, where watching counts as being in. Joining stays optional.
- **let me come and go** — *permission-free circulation.* Paths between zones that don't run past the adult's desk or require a hand raised. The floor plan itself grants the permission to lurk, drop in, and slip away.
- **tell me first** — *a stable map.* Zones that stay put day to day, a visible schedule, changes narrated before they land. Consistency removes the daily cost of re-reading the room.
- **a way out** — *a visible, usable exit from every zone.* Not to leave, necessarily — to know that leaving is allowed. Often that certainty is what makes staying possible. No permission ritual, no penalty for using it.
- **my own spot** — *a claimed base.* A reliable seat, corner, or patch that's theirs, safe from being moved without warning. A fixed point every crossing can return to.

The enclosures the What helps realm names — **a den**, **a corner**, **less to look at**, **a steady sound**, **the right temperature** — are the nest materials themselves. They're covered in the guidebook's What helps note and the budget post; here they're the fabric each crossing is built from.

## On any budget

None of this is a purchase order. A blanket over a table is a cave. A rug zones a watering hole. A turned-around shelf makes a corner. A dimmable lamp makes a decompression seam. A cushion at the join of two zones is a pause pad.

The materials are yours to choose. The card names the need; you fill it with what you have. For the full material-level how-to, see [Creating Cavendish Space on a Budget](https://stimpunks.org/2024/03/14/creating-cavendish-space-on-a-budget/) and [Nesting](https://stimpunks.org/glossary/nesting/).

## How it pairs with the play modes

Two play modes map the rhythm; this layer builds the room that lets the rhythm happen.

Run **Map the edges** *with* the child to find where the crossings actually bite — focus to talking, rest to joining in, home to out-the-door. Run **Moving between** to see the shape of a day, alone to together and back. Then build the padding where the map shows it's needed.

The child maps. You construct. The room changes — not the child.

---- 

## Guardrails

The two rules that hold up the deck hold up the room.

- **Build with the person, never at them.** A padded crossing is designed with the child and offered as a gentle nudge — never sprung as a demand. Designed *with*, not done *to*.
- **The crossing belongs to the environment, not the child.** You are padding the gap, not fixing the person. A hard transition is information about the room, never a verdict on whoever has to cross it.
- **This is not a compliance system.** Smoother transitions are a by-product, never the goal. The goal is a day that's survivable without paying in stress, exhaustion, or shutdown. Don't let "we built them a nook" become a reason to under-provide real rest, real support, or a child's actual communication tools.

Used to route children efficiently through a space that was never made to fit them, this layer becomes the opposite of what it is for.
