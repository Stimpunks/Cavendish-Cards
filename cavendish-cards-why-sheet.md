# Cavendish Cards

*A Why Sheet from Stimpunks Foundation.*

## What This Is

**Cavendish Cards is a free, neuroaffirming deck of pictorial prompt cards.** A child — or anyone — *shows* how they feel and what they need by pointing at a picture or laying a few cards down. No reading required. No scoring, no matching, no right answer, no winning.

- The child controls sharing. A spread laid face-down stays private; turning a card up is the child sharing it. **The turn is the consent.**
- A card names a need, a state, or a change to the environment — **never the child as the problem.**
- The words on each card are for the adults. A child never has to read to play.
- Free, open (CC0), and community-authored. Play it at [cavendish.app](https://cavendish.app/).

A spread is a **design brief for the environment**, not a behavior report. A child laying *cave, buzzy, headphones* has handed you a to-do list for the room.

---

## Why This Exists

Neurodivergent and Disabled children are constantly asked to explain themselves in words they may not have — to justify a need, narrate a feeling, or account for a behavior, on demand, to adults who hold the power in the room. That asymmetry wears children down and often gets their needs misread as misbehavior.

**Cavendish Cards move the work off the child and onto the environment.** They make one hard-to-voice thing — sensory, regulatory, and emotional weather, and the conditions that help — sayable and shareable, on the child's own terms. Regulation comes before instruction. Environment is curriculum. The cards give a child a way to shape the space around them before anyone asks them to perform in it.

## How the Cards Serve Students — In Line With Our Learning Space

Cavendish Cards come out of the [Cavendish Space](https://stimpunks.org/space/) model: psychologically and sensory safe space suited to zone work, flow, intermittent collaboration, and collaborative niche construction. The deck is that philosophy made tangible in a child's hands.

**Self-directed learning.** The cave is the space for quiet reflection and self-directed learning — and the child, not the schedule, decides when to enter it. Across the whole deck the child chooses *which* card, *when*, and *whether* to share it. There is no correct card and no adult-held answer key. The child directs the encounter. (`the cave`, `i need a minute`, `ready now`, and the *show me* way to play.)

**Freedom of embodiment.** Somatic liberty — the freedom to move, stim, and be at home in one's own body — is a first principle of our space, not a privilege to be earned. The deck gives it words: `let me stim`, `room to move`, `busy hands`, `sit my way`, `happy flappy`. When one of these is out, the instruction to the adult is always the same — make room for the movement, and never still the hands.

**Sensory safety.** We take sensory needs seriously in every setting. The *what helps* realm is sensory-safety niche construction, itemized: `headphones`, `dim the light`, `less to look at`, `the right temperature`, `a steady sound`. A card turns an invisible sensory load into a concrete, answerable request.

**Niche construction.** We reshape environments to fit real bodies and minds rather than asking people to conform. *What helps* is the child specifying the niche — `a den`, `a corner`, `a way out`, `let me control it` — and the reshaping is shared work, not the child's burden to carry alone.

**Caves, campfires, and watering holes.** The Places realm *is* the three primordial learning spaces. The child names which one they need right now — `the cave` to withdraw, `the campfire` for a small trusted group, `the watering hole` to be near people without pressure — and moves between them by their own regulation and readiness.

**Intermittent collaboration.** Togetherness works best in doses, on one's own rhythm. `let me come and go`, the interaction badges (`come say hi`, `people i know`, `not right now`), and the *moving between* way to play let a child map and signal that rhythm without explaining or apologizing for it.

**Flow states and monotropism.** Deep, absorbed attention is where much of the best learning happens; interrupting it is costly. `in the zone`, `let me finish`, `no rush`, and `tell me first` protect the attention tunnel and give warning before a transition, so coming up for air happens on the child's terms.

**Psychological and learner safety.** Learner safety means being safe to contribute without fear of being embarrassed, marginalized, or punished. Face-down-by-default, consent-as-the-turn, and the *Kind words* (`you're not broken`, `you belong here`, `nothing to fix`) build the felt safety that everything else rests on.

**Neurological pluralism.** A room holds many bodyminds with real, sometimes conflicting needs. The Growers (`dandelion`, `tulip`, `orchid`) hold differential sensitivity without ranking it, and the *play as the environment* mode lets staff feel plural needs coexisting rather than hearing about them.

**Presumed competence and self-determination.** A spread is self-advocacy in the child's hands — the child is the author of what gets said and shared. That presumes competence by design and hands agency back to the person the room is supposedly for.

## What It Does (and Doesn't Do)

**It does:**

- Make a child's needs sayable without words, and shareable only on the child's terms.
- Turn invisible load into a concrete request the environment can answer.
- Treat access as environmental work, not individual effort.
- Sit *alongside* a child's real communication tools.

**It doesn't:**

- **Screen, score, sort, assess, or diagnose.** A spread is the child telling you something — not you assessing them. Used to rank or flag children, the deck becomes the opposite of what it is for.
- **Replace AAC.** The deck is a lens, not a language. It is small and opinionated on purpose. If a child needs a way to communicate, they need AAC — "they have the cards" is never a reason to under-provide it.
- **Reward, target, or shape behavior.** No card is earned, and turning a card up is communication, not performance. There is no behaviorism here.
- **Name the child as the problem.** If it isn't working, the environment is what hasn't fit yet. Broken systems, not broken people.

## Who This Is For

- **Students** who need to be understood without having to explain in words they may not have.
- **Families and carers** advocating for a child's sensory, regulatory, and interaction needs.
- **Teachers and support staff** who want a spread they can read as a design brief for the room.
- **Anyone building a learning space** that has to work for a plural range of bodyminds.

---

## Why It Matters

When a child's unmet need shows up as behavior, systems tend to manage the behavior and miss the need. Cavendish Cards interrupt that. They give a child a legible, dignified, low-cost way to say *what would help* — and they point the adult at the environment instead of the child. A spread doesn't ask a child to change. It asks the room to.

**Broken systems, not broken people.**

---

## Plain-Language Summary

Cavendish Cards let a child show how they feel and what they need by pointing at pictures — no reading, no scoring, no being tested. The child decides what to share. The cards turn a child's needs into a plan for changing the room, and they line up with how our learning space already works: self-directed learning, freedom to move and stim, sensory safety, and building a space that fits.
