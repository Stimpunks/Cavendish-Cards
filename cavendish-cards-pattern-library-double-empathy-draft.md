1. **Voice.** Written as a Library pattern, Double Empathy must stay counter-deficit and reciprocal — the gap is *between* neurotypes, never a deficit located in Autistic people. The current public page ([glossary: Double Empathy Extreme Problem](https://stimpunks.org/glossary/double-empathy/)) is DEEP-framed and clinical-adjacent; the Library entry should be the calmer, design-facing sibling.
2. **Scope.** If 28 becomes card-nameable, does anything *else* in the Interaction realm re-pattern to it, or does it stay behind those cards (with `say it straight` the one card that names it)? Recommendation below: keep Interaction behind-lens; let `say it straight` be the single card that names 28. One card is enough to graduate a pattern from lens to nameable.

If Helen prefers to keep 28 as a pure behind-lens, nothing in the shipped deck changes — `say it straight` already stands on *30 Communication Bandwidth*.

## Draft — Pattern 28: Double Empathy

*Written in the Library's house format (Opening → The Pattern → Why it matters → Design implications → Patterns above/below → Where it appears → Recipes). This is a proposal, not a published page.*

**Category:** Participation & Collaboration (27–34)
**Status if accepted:** 🟢 publishable · card-nameable

**Problem.** Communication norms are set by one neurotype and treated as neutral, so when meaning fails to cross between different minds, the mismatch is scored as one person's deficit — usually the Autistic person's — instead of as a two-way gap.

**Solution.** Design communication so meaning survives the crossing: make the implicit explicit, hand people more than one channel, and treat a breakdown as a shared repair, not a fault to locate.

### Opening statement

Understanding does not fail because one mind is broken. It fails in the *space between* two minds that process, express, and expect meaning differently. The double empathy problem (Milton, 2012) names this as reciprocal: Autistic and non-Autistic people each find the other hard to read, and each is doing the same amount of interpretive work. The problem is not a lack of empathy in Autistic people; it is a mismatch that both sides feel — and that only one side is usually asked to fix.

### The pattern

- Meaning is carried not only by words but by tone, timing, inference, and shared assumption — and those layers are neurotype-specific. Between different neurotypes, they misfire.
- The misfire is **symmetrical**. Non-Autistic people misread Autistic communication as often as the reverse (Crompton et al., 2020; Davis and Crompton, 2021). Autistic-to-Autistic communication is frequently *more* effective, not less — which locates the gap in the crossing, not in the person.
- Because dominant norms are treated as the neutral baseline, the cost of the mismatch is offloaded onto the non-dominant party: extra decoding effort, uncertainty, and the pressure to mask or adapt.
- Indirect and implied meaning widens the gap; plain, direct, redundant communication narrows it.

### Why this pattern matters

Most "communication support" asks the Autistic person to get better at reading a room built for someone else. That is the deficit model wearing a lanyard. Double Empathy refuses it: if meaning isn't crossing, the *channel* is the thing to redesign, not the person on one end of it. Naming the gap as reciprocal moves the work off the individual and onto the shared communication environment — the same move the whole deck makes. It also protects a real strength: Autistic people communicate well with each other, and mixed-neurotype settings can be designed to keep that intact rather than treating one style as the correction target.

### Design implications

- **Say it straight.** Use plain, direct language. State the request, the feedback, and the expectation without hints or hidden tests. (This is the card.)
- **Make the implicit explicit.** Surface the unwritten norms — timing, turn-taking, what "done" means — instead of assuming they're shared.
- **Offer more than one channel.** Written alongside spoken, with processing time; a second channel is access, not a workaround.
- **Repair, don't attribute.** When a message doesn't land, treat it as a two-way breakdown to fix together, not a fault to locate in one person.
- **Don't tax the non-dominant style.** Stop asking one side to carry all the adaptation. Mixed-neurotype teams share the interpretive work.
